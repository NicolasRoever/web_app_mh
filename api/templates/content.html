Managing Stress and Supporting Your Mental Health

What Is Stress?

Stress is the body’s natural response to challenges or demands in life . It can be triggered by everyday situations – work deadlines, family responsibilities, health concerns – as well as major changes or crises. In the moment, stress can actually help us rise to a challenge. However , too much stress or ongoing (chronic) stress can affect our well-being . It may lead to emotional signs (like feeling anxious, irritable, or sad) and physical signs (such as headaches, trouble sleeping, or tense muscles) . You might notice changes in appetite or difficulty concentrating when stress builds up . The good news is that stress is manageable. It’s something everyone experiences from time to time , and with the right strategies, you can cope with stress and even build resilience. This brochure introduces practical, evidence-based techniques to help you handle stress and support your mental health every day.


Grounding Techniques

When stress feels overwhelming, grounding techniques can bring you back to the present moment and help calm intense feelings. Grounding simply means anchoring yourself in the here and now instead of getting lost in worries about the past or future. Grounding is a skill to “keep us in the present moment” when we’re feeling stressed . Here are a few easy grounding methods:

  • Deep breathing: Take slow, deep breaths. Inhale gently through your nose, and exhale slowly through your mouth. Focus on the feeling of air filling and leaving your lungs. Breathing deeply helps signal your body to relax – it can slow a racing heart and help you return to a calmer state. Even just a few minutes of deep breathing can reduce stress in the middle of a hectic day.
  • 5-4-3-2-1 sensory exercise: This is a quick grounding exercise you can do anywhere. Look around and name 5 things you can see, feel 4 things you can touch (your chair, the floor, your hands…), listen for 3 sounds you can hear, notice 2 things you can smell, and taste 1 thing (even just paying attention to the taste in your mouth). Going through these five steps engages your senses and pulls your mind back to the present when anxiety has you distracted. It’s a simple technique that many therapists recommend for moments of panic or worry.
  • Grounding through touch or movement: If you feel disoriented by stress, try a physical action to ground yourself. For example, place your feet flat on the floor and notice the support of the ground. You can also gently press your hands together or give yourself a hug. Some people find it helpful to carry a small object (like a smooth stone or stress ball) to hold and squeeze when feeling tense – the sensation can be calming and remind you that you’re right here, right now. Taking a brief walk and noticing each step can also reconnect you with your body and the present.

Each of these grounding techniques is easy to practice and can be done in just a few minutes. They are like mental “reset” buttons, shifting your focus away from racing thoughts to something more steady and real (your breath, your senses, the world around you). Try to practice a grounding exercise when you’re only mildly stressed, so that it will be easier to use in a moment of high stress. Over time, these techniques can become healthy habits you automatically turn to whenever you need to find calm.


Habits for Stress Management

One of the best ways to manage stress is to take care of your overall well-being . Healthy daily habits can make you more resilient to stress and improve your mood. Here are some evidence-based healthy habits to support your mental health:

  • Stick to a sleep routine: Getting enough sleep is crucial for your mind and body. Most adults need around 7 or more hours of sleep per night. Try to go to bed and wake up at the same time each day. Good sleep restores your energy and helps you cope with challenges better. If stress makes it hard to sleep, create a calming bedtime routine (for example, dim the lights, avoid screens before bed, or try a few minutes of deep breathing).
  • Get regular exercise: Staying active can lift your mood and reduce stress. Even gentle exercise releases tension and prompts your body to produce feel-good chemicals. You don’t need to be an athlete – start with small activities you enjoy. Something as simple as a 30-minute walk each day can boost your mood and health. Find an activity that you like (walking the dog, dancing to music, yoga, cycling, etc.) and make it a routine. Over time, regular movement makes you less prone to stress and anxiety.
  • Eat balanced, regular meals: Your brain and body handle stress better when nourished. Try to have a healthy, balanced diet with plenty of fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and proteins. Stay hydrated by drinking enough water. Avoid excessive caffeine or sugar, which can spike anxiety or mood swings. And while alcohol or comfort foods might feel like stress relief in the moment, they often make things worse later – so use them in moderation if at all. Paying attention to nutrition can improve your energy and focus, making it easier to deal with stress.
  • Make time to unwind: Relaxation is not a luxury; it’s part of good mental health. Schedule a little time each day (even 15 minutes) for an activity that relaxes you or makes you happy. This could be reading, listening to music, taking a warm bath, spending time in nature, or practicing a hobby. Doing something enjoyable and calming resets your stress levels. Some people also find relaxation techniques like meditation, gentle stretching, or progressive muscle relaxation helpful – these practices can lower your body’s stress response over time. Find what works for you and make it a regular part of your routine.
  • Connect with others: You don’t have to handle stress alone. Reaching out to friends, family, or supportive people can lighten your emotional load. Talking with someone you trust about what you’re feeling can provide comfort and perspective. Even a quick phone call or chat over coffee with a friend can remind you that people care about you. Connecting with others – whether it’s friends, family, colleagues, or a community group – builds a support network that buffers against stress. Consider also spending time with pets if you have them; the companionship of animals can be very soothing.
  • Practice gratitude and positive thinking: When stress is high, our minds often fixate on negatives. Consciously reminding yourself of things you’re grateful for can counteract this tendency. Try thinking of a few good things each day, no matter how small – a funny moment, a kind gesture, or simply a pleasant meal. Writing these down in a journal can reinforce positive feelings. In fact, practicing gratitude daily can improve your well-being and reduce stress. Along with gratitude, try to notice positive moments and accomplishments (“What went right today?”). This isn’t about ignoring problems, but about balancing your perspective so that fears or setbacks don’t fill your whole mind.
  • Set realistic goals and boundaries: Sometimes stress comes from feeling overwhelmed by tasks and responsibilities. It’s important to prioritize and set boundaries to protect your mental health. Make a habit of planning your day or week and deciding what tasks are most important, and what can wait. Learn to say “no” when you have too much on your plate – it’s okay to decline additional commitments or ask for help. By focusing on what truly matters and letting go of less important pressures, you reduce unnecessary stress. At day’s end, instead of dwelling on what you didn’t finish, remind yourself of what you did accomplish, no matter how small. Celebrating small wins helps maintain a positive mindset.

Each of these healthy habits reinforces your mental and physical resilience. You don’t have to change everything at once – small steps make a difference . Pick one or two habits to focus on, and gradually build on your successes. Over time, these habits become part of your lifestyle, acting as a “buffer” that makes you stronger in the face of stress . Remember , taking care of your body (sleep, exercise, nutrition) and your mind (relaxation, social connection, gratitude) go hand in hand in managing stress. By nurturing yourself daily, you’ll find you have more energy, a clearer head, and a calmer outlook when challenges arise.


Coping with Thoughts and Emotions

Stress isn’t just what happens to us – it’s also influenced by how we think and how we react emotionally. Two people might face the same situation and feel differently based on their thoughts. That’s why learning to manage our thoughts and feelings is a powerful stress strategy. This section covers some simple techniques from psychology (including cognitive-behavioral therapy and mindfulness practices) to help you navigate tough thoughts and emotions in a healthy way:


  • Notice and name what you feel: One mindfulness technique the WHO guide suggests is “Notice and Name.” This means, rather than immediately reacting to a stressful feeling, pause and observe it. Put a name to what you’re feeling (for example: “I notice that I’m feeling anxious” or “I’m having the thought that I might fail this test”). Gently labeling your feelings or thoughts can actually lessen their grip. It might sound simple, but when you acknowledge a feeling, you’re stepping back from it just a little – enough to get some perspective. Psychologists find that accepting and observing emotions without judgment (instead of fighting them) helps them pass more quickly and improves emotional balance. In other words, when a wave of stress hits, see if you can mentally say “This is a wave of stress” – remind yourself that it’s a feeling that will rise and eventually fall, not a permanent state. Allowing yourself to feel an emotion (rather than trying to deny or suppress it) often makes it less intense in the long run.
  • Unhook from unhelpful thoughts: Stressful times often come with negative thoughts – harsh self-criticism, worry about “what ifs,” or thinking the worst. A useful skill from cognitive-behavioral therapy is learning to challenge and reframe negative thoughts. Start by recognizing when you have an unkind or unhelpful thought (for example, “I always mess up” or “Everything is going wrong”). Then, question that thought: Is it really true? Is there another way to look at the situation? What would I say to a good friend if they had this thought? This is similar to what the WHO guide calls “unhooking from unkind thoughts” – basically, not letting negative thoughts dictate how you feel about yourself. For instance, if you think “I’m a failure,” unhook by telling yourself something more realistic and compassionate, like “I had a setback, but everyone makes mistakes – I can learn from this.” By treating your thoughts as opinions or mental events (and not absolute truths), you take away some of their power. Over time, practicing this can train your mind to be kinder and more balanced. Remember, don’t believe everything you think – especially when you’re stressed, your mind may be painting a darker picture than reality. If you identify a pattern of negative self-talk, try countering it with more supportive thoughts or affirmations. This is a habit you can build with practice, and it’s at the heart of many evidence-based therapies for stress and anxiety.
  • Practice self-compassion: In challenging times, it’s crucial to be kind to yourself. Many of us are our own worst critics, especially under stress. Self-compassion means treating yourself with the same understanding and care you’d offer to a friend. If you’re feeling overwhelmed, instead of saying “I should be handling this better,” try telling yourself, “This is tough, and I’m doing my best.” Speak to yourself in encouraging words. Remind yourself of your strengths and the progress you’ve made. By building yourself up instead of tearing yourself down, you create an internal environment that’s much more resistant to stress. Self-compassion isn’t selfish or indulgent; it’s actually linked with greater emotional resilience and mental health.
  • Make room for all feelings (acceptance): It’s okay to feel what you feel. Stress can bring out tough emotions – fear , anger , sadness. While it’s not fun to experience them, one of the healthiest things you can do is let those emotions be, without guilt. This concept is sometimes called “making room” for your feelings. It means accepting emotions as natural , moving through you, rather than trying to block them. For example, if you’re anxious about something, you might notice the tightness in your stomach and racing thoughts and think “I shouldn’t feel this way.” But fighting anxiety often adds a second layer of stress (now you’re stressed about being stressed! ). Instead, see if you can take a few slow breaths and internally say, “I’m feeling anxious, and that’s understandable. I can allow this feeling for now; it will pass.” Research suggests that people who practice accepting their negative emotions tend to experience better mental health and less distress over time . Acceptance doesn’t mean you like the feeling or that you want it to stay – it just means you acknowledge it without panicking or judging yourself. Often, when you do that, the emotion will soften on its own. Some strategies to help accept feelings include journaling about what you feel, talking to someone you trust, or using creative outlets (art, music) to express emotions. All of these give your feelings a release so they don’t stay bottled up. By using these strategies – mindfulness of your thoughts, challenging negative thinking, being kind to yourself, and accepting emotions – you are essentially training your mind to respond to stress in a healthier way. These are learned skills, so don’t worry if it feels a bit awkward at first. With practice, you’ll find that you can face stressful situations with more calm and clarity. Remember , thoughts and feelings come and go ; you have the strength to observe them, gently guide them, and not let them overpower you.

When to Seek Help

While stress is a normal part of life, sometimes it can grow beyond what we can handle on our own. Reaching out for help is a sign of strength, not weakness. If you find that your stress or mood is interfering with your ability to live your life or lasting for a long time without relief, it may be time to seek additional support.

  • Persistent symptoms: If you have stress or sadness that lasts more than a couple of weeks and doesn’t seem to improve, take note. For example, ongoing trouble sleeping, significant changes in appetite or weight, feeling exhausted and struggling to get out of bed day after day, or a continued sense of hopelessness are warning signs. Short-term stress is common, but stress that never lets up could be a sign of something more serious, like an anxiety disorder or depression, which are treatable with professional help.
  • Impact on daily life: When stress makes it hard to function – perhaps you can’t concentrate at work or school, you’re neglecting responsibilities, or you no longer enjoy activities you used to – it’s important to reach out. Maybe you find yourself withdrawing from friends or family because you feel overwhelmed, or your performance at work is suffering due to constant worry. These kinds of impacts suggest that extra support is needed.
  • Physical symptoms or health concerns: Chronic stress can manifest physically. If you’re experiencing symptoms like constant fatigue, frequent headaches or stomach aches, or any other unexplained health issues, discuss them with a healthcare provider. Sometimes ruling out medical causes and addressing stress can greatly improve physical symptoms. Always listen to your body – severe stress can raise blood pressure, weaken the immune system, and contribute to other health problems. Don’t hesitate to seek a medical or mental health professional’s guidance if your body is showing signs of distress.
  • You feel overwhelmed or out of control: If you reach a point where coping day-to-day feels impossible, or you’re using unhealthy coping methods (like misusing alcohol or drugs) to get through, please seek help right away. No one has to handle these feelings alone. There are professionals – counselors, therapists, doctors – who are trained to help you find relief and strategies that work for your situation. Sometimes even a few sessions with a therapist or joining a support group can make a big difference in how you feel.
  • Thoughts of self-harm or hopelessness: If you ever have thoughts of hurting yourself or believe that life isn’t worth living, it’s crucial to get help immediately. These feelings can be overwhelming, but they can be treated. Reach out to a mental health crisis line or tell someone you trust who can assist you in finding help. In many countries, there are 24/7 crisis hotlines (for example, in the U.S. you can call or text 988 to reach trained counselors). Professionals are available at any time to talk with you and keep you safe. You are not alone, and suicidal thoughts do not mean something is wrong with you – they are a sign to reach out for more support.

Remember: Asking for help is a brave and positive step. Just as you would see a doctor for a prolonged fever, you should consider seeing a mental health professional for prolonged or intense stress. Therapists and counselors can provide personalized strategies (like talk therapy, cognitive-behavioral techniques, or even medications if appropriate) to help you feel better. There is no need to wait until a crisis – you can seek help any time you feel you’re struggling more than you want to. Even talking to your regular doctor about stress, or a school counselor if you’re a student, is a good start; they can direct you to resources and support.


Lastly, keep in mind that reaching out for help is a form of self-care and strength. Everyone needs help at times, and getting support can help you get back to feeling like yourself. Whether it’s speaking with a professional, joining a stress management class, or confiding in a support group or community service, help is available and effective. You deserve support and feeling better.


In Conclusion

Managing stress and supporting your mental health is a journey – but one you don’t have to walk alone. By using the strategies in this guide – grounding yourself in the present, building healthy habits, being mindful of your thoughts, and treating yourself with kindness – you are taking important steps toward well-being. Start small, be patient with yourself, and celebrate each positive change you make. Over time, these little daily practices add up to a big difference in how you feel. Life will always have some stress, but with these tools, you can handle challenges with more calm and confidence. And when things feel like too much, remember that help is available. You are strong for caring about your mental health, and you have many resources (and people) ready to support you. You’ve got this, and you’re not alone.
