What is stress?

Stress is the body’s and mind’s response to demands—big or small. Short bursts can help you meet challenges, but long-lasting (chronic) stress may affect mood, focus, sleep, and physical well-being. The goal isn’t to eliminate stress, but to handle it skillfully and return to balance.

  • Common signs: worry, irritability, low mood, muscle tension, headaches, poor sleep, changes in appetite, difficulty concentrating.
  • Key idea: Everyone experiences stress at times. Small, consistent skills and habits make a big difference.

Grounding techniques

When emotions feel like a storm, grounding brings you back to the present moment so you can act on what matters. Try one of these quick practices:

30-second “Notice–Slow–Refocus”

  1. Notice what you’re thinking and feeling (e.g., “I notice tightness in my chest”).
  2. Slow down & connect with your body: gently exhale, press feet into the floor, stretch your hands/arms.
  3. Refocus on the world around you—name what you can see, hear, touch, smell, and taste.

Calming breath (gentle & slow)

Breathe out softly, then let the lungs refill slowly. Keep the breath gentle (not deep or forceful). If you feel light-headed, breathe more shallowly and slow your pace.

The 5-4-3-2-1 senses reset

Silently name: 5 things you can see, 4 you can feel, 3 you can hear, 2 you can smell, 1 you can taste.

Practice grounding when only mildly stressed so it’s easier to use during intense moments.

Healthy habits that buffer stress

  • Sleep: Aim for a steady schedule and a wind-down routine (dimmer lights, screens off, brief breathing or stretch).
  • Move your body: Walk, cycle, dance, or do yoga most days—little and often beats all-or-nothing.
  • Nourish: Regular meals, plenty of plants, protein, whole grains; steady hydration; go easy on caffeine/alcohol.
  • Unwind daily: Micro-breaks matter—music, nature, a hobby, warm shower, gentle stretches, short meditation.
  • Connect: Talk with supportive people; small moments of connection reduce stress and protect mood.
  • Gratitude & small wins: Note 1–3 things that went well; recognize progress, not perfection.
  • Boundaries & pacing: Prioritize, say no when needed, and take tasks one step at a time.

Working with thoughts & emotions

Notice & name

When a tough feeling shows up, label it kindly (e.g., “I’m noticing anxiety”). Naming creates a little space, making feelings easier to ride out.

Unhook from unhelpful thoughts

Treat thoughts as mental events, not facts. If you catch “I always mess up,” try “This was a setback; I can learn the next step.” Ask: What would I say to a friend?

Self-kindness

Speak to yourself like you would to someone you care about: “This is hard—and I’m doing my best.” Self-compassion supports motivation and resilience.

Make room for feelings

Allow feelings to be present (they ebb and flow). Breathe, feel your feet on the ground, and carry on with small, valued actions.

Act on your values

Choose a tiny action that aligns with who you want to be (e.g., 10 minutes reading with a child; sending a kind message). Small steps, repeated, build meaningful change.

When to seek help

  • Stress, anxiety, or low mood most days for several weeks without relief.
  • Sleep problems, major appetite changes, persistent physical tension or pain.
  • Struggling to function at work/school/home or withdrawing from people.
  • Using alcohol or drugs to cope.
  • Thoughts of self-harm or that life isn’t worth living: seek urgent help now through local emergency services or crisis lines.

Support works—talk with your GP/primary care clinician or a licensed mental health professional. You don’t have to handle this alone.
